## Overview
Stapelia gigantea, also known as the Giant Toad Cactus, is a succulent plant native to southern Africa. It is known for its unique, star-shaped flowers that can grow up to 12 inches in diameter and its ability to thrive in indoor conditions with minimal care. The plant's scientific name, Stapelia gigantea, is derived from the Greek word "stapelia," meaning "a cluster of grapes," and the Latin word "gigantea," meaning "giant." Stapelia gigantea is a popular choice among plant enthusiasts due to its low-maintenance requirements and its ability to purify the air, making it an excellent addition to any indoor space.

The plant's native habitat is in the arid regions of southern Africa, where it grows in well-draining soil and full sun. However, when grown indoors, it prefers bright, indirect light and consistent temperatures between 65-80°F (18-27°C). Stapelia gigantea is a relatively small plant, growing up to 12 inches tall and 24 inches wide, making it an ideal choice for small spaces or containers.

## Light: The Importance of Bright, Indirect Light
Stapelia gigantea prefers bright, indirect light, such as 200-400 foot-candles, to photosynthesize and grow. The best window orientation for this plant is east- or west-facing, as it provides the right amount of light without causing the plant to become scorched. In the Northern Hemisphere, it is best to place the plant 3 feet from a south-facing window to avoid direct sunlight. During the winter months, when the sun is lower in the sky, it may be necessary to move the plant closer to the window to ensure it receives enough light.

## Watering: How to Water Stapelia gigantea Without Drowning It
To water Stapelia gigantea, it is essential to check the soil moisture by sticking your finger into the soil up to the first knuckle. If the soil feels dry, it is time to water. Water the plant every 7-10 days during the summer and every 14 days during the winter, making sure to avoid getting water on the leaves or crown to prevent rot. It is also essential to use room-temperature water and avoid getting the plant's roots too wet, as this can cause root rot.

## Soil & Repotting: The Importance of Well-Draining Soil
Stapelia gigantea prefers well-draining soil, such as a mix of 60% potting mix, 30% perlite, and 10% orchid bark. This soil mix will help prevent water from accumulating in the soil and reduce the risk of root rot. It is also essential to use a terracotta pot, as it allows for better drainage and aeration. Repotting is necessary when the plant becomes pot-bound, which can be identified by roots growing out of the drainage holes or the plant becoming top-heavy.

## Fertilizing
To fertilize Stapelia gigantea, use a balanced 10-10-10 fertilizer at half strength, applied monthly during the spring and summer months. This will provide the plant with the necessary nutrients to grow and thrive.

## Humidity and Temperature
The ideal humidity range for Stapelia gigantea is 50-60%, which can be achieved by using a pebble tray or grouping plants. The temperature range should be between 65-80°F (18-27°C), avoiding extreme temperatures that can cause damage to the plant.

## Toxicity & Safety: Non-Toxic to Cats and Dogs
According to the ASPCA, Stapelia gigantea is non-toxic to cats and dogs. However, it is still essential to keep the plant out of reach of pets to avoid accidental ingestion. If ingested, the plant may cause mild gastrointestinal upset, but it is not toxic.

### Overwatering
Overwatering is one of the most common problems with Stapelia gigantea. To fix this issue, stop watering and repot the plant in well-draining soil. It is also essential to check the soil moisture before watering to prevent water from accumulating in the soil.

### Underwatering
Underwatering can also cause problems for Stapelia gigantea. To fix this issue, water the plant thoroughly and adjust the watering schedule to ensure the soil is moist but not waterlogged.

### Too Much Direct Sunlight
Too much direct sunlight can cause the leaves to become brown and crispy. To fix this issue, move the plant to a spot with bright, indirect light, and use a sheer curtain to filter direct sunlight.

### Pests
Pests can infest Stapelia gigantea, causing damage to the plant. To fix this issue, treat the plant with insecticidal soap and inspect the plant regularly for signs of pests.

### Slow Growth
Slow growth can be caused by a lack of nutrients. To fix this issue, fertilize the plant with a balanced fertilizer and adjust the fertilization schedule to ensure the plant is receiving the necessary nutrients.

## Propagation
To propagate Stapelia gigantea, follow these steps:
1. Cut a stem section from the mother plant, making sure it has at least two nodes.
2. Remove lower leaves from the stem section, leaving only the top two or three leaves.
3. Allow the cut end to dry for 24-48 hours to form a callus.
4. Plant the stem section in well-draining soil, making sure the node is above the soil surface.
5. Water sparingly until roots develop, which can take 1-3 months.
