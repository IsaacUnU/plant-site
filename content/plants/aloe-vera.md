## Quick Care Summary

Aloe vera is a succulent — not a cactus — that evolved in the dry margins of East Africa and the Arabian Peninsula. It has been in continuous human cultivation for over 6,000 years. Ancient Egyptians documented its use on papyrus. What makes it genuinely worth growing is not just the architecture of its rosette but the two chemically distinct products locked inside every leaf: a clear gel with real clinical efficacy for minor burns, and a yellow latex that is genuinely hazardous and that many care guides bury in a footnote.

The single most common way to kill aloe vera is watering it like a tropical houseplant. The second most common way is putting it outdoors in summer sun without acclimatisation. Both are entirely preventable if you understand the plant's biology.

## Where This Plant Comes From

Aloe vera originated along the arid coasts of East Africa, the Arabian Peninsula, and the Canary Islands. It grows in shallow, rocky soil where rainfall is unpredictable and draining fast is a survival skill rather than an advantage. The thick leaves are not just storage tanks — they are reservoirs maintained under mild positive pressure, which is why a healthy aloe leaf snaps cleanly when cut rather than wilting.

Human cultivation began at least 6,000 years ago. Egyptian, Mesopotamian, Greek, and Roman texts all document medicinal use. The Greek physician Dioscorides described it in *De Materia Medica* (circa 77 AD) for wound healing. Today, aloe is grown commercially across tropical and subtropical regions, and the global aloe vera market is valued at over £400 million annually — driven primarily by the cosmetics and pharmaceutical sectors, not the houseplant trade.

Understanding the plant's natural environment explains almost every care decision. It wants sun, wants fast drainage, and actively prefers to dry out between waterings. The soil in its native range is essentially mineral grit. When we grow it in standard houseplant compost and water it weekly, we are replicating the worst possible conditions for its root system.

## Light: Bright and Direct, But Introduced Carefully

Aloe vera is a high-light plant. The optimal range is 800–1,000 foot-candles, which corresponds to a south or southwest-facing windowsill in the UK, or six or more hours of direct sun per day. In these conditions the plant grows steadily, the leaves stay firm and upright, and the rosette maintains its characteristic blue-green colour.

At 400 foot-candles — medium indirect light — the plant survives but grows slowly. The leaves become slightly elongated as the plant reaches toward the light source, a response called etiolation. Below 200 foot-candles, the leaves turn pale, translucent, and eventually soft and watery. An aloe kept in a dark corner does not slowly decline: it appears to melt. I have seen it happen within three months in a north-facing office where the desk was more than two metres from the window.

If you are moving a plant that has spent the winter indoors onto a sunny balcony or into a garden in May, you need to acclimate it. The epidermis of an indoor aloe has not had time to develop the protective pigmentation (the red-orange flush you see on sun-stressed plants) that buffers against UV intensity. Put it in direct sun immediately and the leaf tips will turn brown and crisp within days — that damage is irreversible. The fix is gradual: add fifteen minutes of direct sun exposure per day over two weeks before leaving it in full outdoor sun.

The reddening of aloe leaves in intense light is not injury — it is the plant producing anthocyanins as a sunscreen. It is not a sign that you need to move it. Bleached patches that turn white or grey-brown, on the other hand, indicate actual tissue damage from too-rapid sun exposure.

## Watering: The Bottom-of-the-Pot Rule

Virtually every overwatered aloe I have encountered belonged to someone who was told to "let the soil dry out before watering." They interpreted this as "dry on the surface." Aloe vera roots are adapted to periods of complete drought. The substrate needs to dry out fully — not just the top inch, but through to the bottom of the pot.

The way I test this: push a wooden skewer or your finger down the side of the pot to the drainage layer. If it comes out with any soil clinging to it, wait longer. When in doubt, wait an extra week. In my experience, the most reliable watering schedule for UK indoor conditions is every two to three weeks in summer (when the heating is off and humidity is higher) and every six to eight weeks in winter (when central heating creates very dry conditions and growth slows dramatically).

When you do water, water thoroughly — add water slowly until it runs freely from the drainage holes. Then stop and do not water again until the test above confirms complete dryness.

The classic symptoms of overwatering appear at the base: leaves become soft, translucent, and brown at the point where they meet the stem. If you catch this early (outer leaves affected, inner leaves still firm), unpot the plant, cut away all soft root and stem tissue with a clean blade, dust the wounds with powdered cinnamon (a natural antifungal) or sulphur, let the plant sit unpotted in a dry spot for three to five days, then repot in fresh dry grit mix. Do not water for ten days after repotting.

## Soil and Repotting

The mix I use for every aloe in my care: 60% proprietary cactus compost and 40% horticultural perlite or coarse grit (not builder's sand — it compacts). This provides the fast drainage the plant requires while retaining enough organic matter for slow, steady nutrient release.

Standard houseplant compost alone retains too much moisture and will cause root rot within eighteen months, even if you water carefully. The compost sits wet for too long between watering events, and aloe roots are not designed for that.

Terracotta pots are strongly preferable to plastic or ceramic. Clay is porous — it wicks excess moisture away from the root zone and allows the substrate to dry faster. A plastic pot that is exactly the right size will hold moisture noticeably longer than the same terracotta pot. This is not trivial for aloe.

Repot when you see one of two signals: pups crowding the pot to the point where watering becomes difficult, or roots clearly emerging from the drainage holes for several months. Aloe does not mind being root-bound. Do not move up more than one pot size at a time — a too-large pot holds too much soil volume relative to the roots, and that extra volume stays wet, increasing rot risk.

## Fertilizing

Aloe vera evolved in nutrient-poor soil. It does not need regular feeding. In the growing season (April through August in the UK) I apply a half-strength balanced liquid fertiliser twice — once in April and once in June. Nothing more. Over-fertilised aloe produces fast, soft growth that is structurally weak and more susceptible to rot.

In autumn and winter, stop feeding entirely. Dormant plants cannot process fertiliser efficiently, and excess nitrogen in cold wet compost encourages root decay.

## Humidity and Temperature

Aloe vera is a xeric plant. It prefers dry air (30–40% relative humidity) and will not thank you for misting. Most UK homes are well within this range, especially in winter with central heating running. This is one of the few indoor plants that benefits from dry central heating.

Temperature-wise, the comfort range is 55–80°F (13–27°C). Below 50°F (10°C) the plant enters a stress state — growth stops and the leaves may develop water-soaked spots. A single night of frost causes cell rupture in the leaves: they go translucent, then mushy, then dead. If you have your aloe outside in summer, bring it in by early September in most UK regions — do not wait for the first frost warning.

## How to Propagate Aloe Vera

Aloe vera propagates almost exclusively through offsets, called pups. These emerge from the base of the plant as the root system matures — a single well-established plant in a large pot can produce five or six pups in a good growing season.

The rule I follow: do not separate a pup until it has at least three of its own leaves and you can feel a distinct resistance when you gently tug it, indicating that it has developed its own root system. A pup removed before this stage has no way to absorb water while it establishes and will simply desiccate.

To separate: unpot the entire plant. Find where the pup connects to the parent stem — it is usually a thin horizontal runner called a stolon. Cut it cleanly with a sharp, sterile blade. Do not pull or twist. Let the cut surface on both the pup and the parent sit in the open air for at least 24 hours, ideally 48. The wound needs to callous over before contact with moist soil; this step prevents the bacterial and fungal infections that most propagation failures trace back to.

Pot the callused pup in dry cactus mix. Wait a week before the first watering. The plant does not need water to establish roots — it has enough water stored in its leaves to sustain itself for a week while new root tips grow.

## Toxicity and Pet Safety

Aloe vera is toxic to cats and dogs, according to the ASPCA. The toxic compounds are saponins and anthraquinones, present throughout the plant but concentrated in the yellow latex layer between the green outer skin and the clear interior gel. Symptoms in pets include vomiting, diarrhoea, lethargy, and urine that appears red or orange in colour due to oxidised anthraquinone metabolites.

This is worth knowing clearly: the *gel* — the clear, mucilaginous centre of a fresh leaf — is distinct from the *latex*. The gel contains acemannan (a polysaccharide), along with vitamins C and E, zinc, and various enzymes. Research, including studies from the Centre for Advances in Cancer Education, has documented its efficacy on minor first and second-degree burns and wound healing. The latex is a completely different substance with laxative properties and is not safe for internal consumption by humans or pets.

If you have cats or dogs, either keep aloe in a room they cannot access or choose a different plant entirely. There are no safe arrangements that involve a curious cat and a pot on a low shelf.

## Harvesting Aloe Gel: The Correct Method

Most tutorials skim over this. Here is the accurate process:

1. Choose a leaf from the outermost ring of the rosette — the oldest, thickest leaves. Never take inner leaves; they are the plant's active growth.
2. Cut the selected leaf at the base, as close to the stem as possible, using a sharp clean knife. Do not saw — one clean cut minimises the wound.
3. Stand the cut leaf upright, cut end down, in a glass or cup. Leave it for ten minutes. You will see yellow liquid draining out of the cut end. This is the latex. You want to drain as much of this as possible before using the leaf.
4. Rinse the cut end under cool running water.
5. Lay the leaf flat, slice off the serrated edges, then cut through the flat faces to open the leaf. The clear gel is between the green skin layers.
6. Scoop or scrape the gel out. Use it directly on minor burns, sunburn, or dry skin.

The gel can be refrigerated for up to seven days in a sealed container. I prefer to freeze it in ice cube trays — frozen cubes can be stored for up to two months and applied directly to sunburned skin where the cold provides additional relief.

Do not blend aloe gel if you intend to use it on open wounds — the mechanical disruption can introduce the residual latex from the skin edges into the gel. For a clean medicinal product, scoop only from the centre.

## Where to Buy and What to Look For

Aloe vera is one of the most widely available houseplants in the UK. You will find it in supermarkets, garden centres, DIY stores, and online. Prices vary significantly by size:

- Small (10 cm across): £2–5
- Medium (20–30 cm): £8–15
- Large, established plant with multiple pups: £20–40

When selecting a plant, look for firm, upright leaves without soft spots. Avoid plants with brown or mushy bases — this is root rot already in progress and almost impossible to reverse in small plants. Pups attached to the main plant are a sign of a healthy, established root system. The soil in the pot should feel dry to the touch — avoid any plant sitting in waterlogged compost.

A word on supermarket aloe: the plants are often potted in standard compost with no drainage layer and sit in decorative pots with no drainage holes. Repot immediately on arrival.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**I've had my aloe for two years and it has never produced any pups. What's wrong?**

Most likely it needs a larger pot or better light. Pups are produced when the plant is mature, well-rooted, and growing actively. If the rosette is still small (under 20 cm across), the plant may simply not have reached maturity. If it is large but produces nothing, check that it is receiving at least six hours of bright light per day — aloe rarely offsets in low light — and consider repotting up one size in spring.

**My aloe's leaves are turning orange-red. Is it stressed?**

Not necessarily. Aloe vera produces anthocyanin pigments in response to high UV light, temperature stress, or nutrient deficiency. A slight reddening in summer sun is normal and not harmful. If the entire plant turns a deep rust-red and the leaves feel firm, try moving it out of the most intense afternoon sun. If the leaves are also soft, check for root issues.

**Can I use aloe gel from my houseplant on my face?**

The gel from a fresh leaf is generally safe for external use on intact skin. The main concern is incomplete removal of the yellow latex, which can cause skin irritation in some people. Follow the draining and rinsing steps above carefully. For repeated use or on sensitive skin, I would recommend a commercially prepared, triple-filtered aloe gel where the latex has been removed by processing.

**I've been told aloe cleans indoor air. Is that true?**

Aloe vera was included in NASA's 1989 Clean Air Study as one of the plants capable of reducing formaldehyde and benzene in sealed test chambers. The practical implication for a normal room is modest — you would need many plants to produce measurable air quality improvement. The study is real; the marketing claims built on it are often exaggerated. Grow aloe for its other qualities.

**How do I know when my aloe definitely needs water?**

The definitive test is to push a wooden skewer to the bottom of the pot. Dry soil will leave the skewer clean. Any soil adhering means the substrate is still moist enough. If you cannot use a skewer, lift the pot — a pot of dry cactus mix is noticeably lighter than the same pot recently watered. Weight is often more reliable than visual inspection for succulents.
