## Light: The Window Direction That Determines Passiflora caerulea's Fate
Passiflora caerulea prefers bright, indirect light, ideally between 200-400 foot-candles. The best window orientation for this plant is east- or west-facing, as it provides the ideal amount of light without causing the leaves to become scorched. In the Northern Hemisphere, it's best to place the plant 3-5 feet away from the window to provide the ideal amount of light. During the winter months, when the sun is lower in the sky, the plant may require more frequent pruning to maintain its shape and promote healthy growth.

## Watering: How to Water Passiflora caerulea Without Drowning It
To check the soil moisture, stick your finger into the soil up to the first knuckle. If the soil feels dry, it's time to water. Water the plant every 7-10 days in the summer and every 14 days in the winter. Make sure to use room temperature water, as cold water can shock the roots. It's also important to avoid getting water on the leaves to prevent fungal diseases. If you notice the plant is droopy or the leaves are yellowing, it may be a sign of overwatering or underwatering.

## Soil & Repotting: The Right Soil Mix Makes Root Rot Rare
The ideal soil mix for Passiflora caerulea is a well-draining mix that contains 60% potting mix, 30% perlite, and 10% orchid bark. This mix will help prevent waterlogged soil and root rot. When repotting, choose a pot that is only 1-2 sizes larger than the current one, as Passiflora caerulea prefers to be slightly root-bound. The best time to repot is in the spring, when the plant is actively growing.

## Fertilizing
Feed the plant with a balanced 10-10-10 fertilizer, diluted to half the recommended strength, once a month during the spring and summer. This will provide the necessary nutrients for healthy growth and flowering. Avoid fertilizing during the winter months, as the plant is dormant and doesn't require as many nutrients.

## Humidity and Temperature
The ideal humidity level for Passiflora caerulea is between 50-70%. To maintain this level, you can use a humidifier or place the plant on a tray filled with water and pebbles. The ideal temperature range is between 65-80°F (18-27°C). Keep the plant away from heating vents and cold windows to maintain a consistent temperature.

## Toxicity & Safety: The Importance of Placement
According to the ASPCA, Passiflora caerulea is non-toxic to cats and dogs, making it a great choice for families with pets. However, it's still important to keep the plant out of reach of children and pets, as the plant's sap can cause skin irritation in some individuals. If you suspect your pet has ingested any part of the plant, contact the ASPCA Animal Poison Control Center at (888) 426-4435.

## Common Problems
### Soft, Mushy Stem Base
Symptom: The stem base of the plant is soft and mushy to the touch.
Cause: Overwatering or root rot.
Fix: Stop watering the plant immediately and allow the soil to dry out completely. Prune any affected areas, and repot the plant in a well-draining mix.

### Yellowing Leaves
Symptom: The leaves of the plant are yellowing or dropping.
Cause: Underwatering or overwatering.
Fix: Check the soil moisture and adjust the watering schedule accordingly. Make sure to provide the plant with enough light and nutrients.

### Pests
Symptom: The plant is infested with pests, such as mealybugs or spider mites.
Cause: Poor air circulation or contaminated soil.
Fix: Isolate the plant and treat it with insecticidal soap or neem oil. Improve air circulation around the plant, and repot it in a well-draining mix.

### Slow Growth
Symptom: The plant is not growing as quickly as expected.
Cause: Lack of light, nutrients, or water.
Fix: Provide the plant with more light, fertilize it regularly, and adjust the watering schedule accordingly.

## Propagation
To propagate Passiflora caerulea, follow these steps:
1. Cut a stem section from the mother plant, making sure it has at least two nodes.
2. Remove lower leaves from the cutting, leaving only the top two or three leaves.
3. Dip the cut end of the stem in rooting hormone powder.
4. Plant the cutting in a well-draining mix, making sure the node is buried in the soil.
5. Water the soil gently but thoroughly, and provide the cutting with bright, indirect light.
6. Keep the soil consistently moist but not waterlogged, and roots should develop within 1-2 months.

## Frequently Asked Questions
**How often should I water my Passiflora caerulea?**
Water your Passiflora caerulea every 7-10 days in the summer and every 14 days in the winter, making sure not to overwater.

**Is Passiflora caerulea toxic to cats and dogs?**

**Why are the leaves on my Passiflora caerulea turning yellow?**
Yellowing leaves can be a sign of underwatering or overwatering. Check the soil moisture and adjust the watering schedule accordingly.

**Can Passiflora caerulea grow in low light?**
While Passiflora caerulea can tolerate low light, it prefers bright, indirect light. If you're looking to grow the plant in low light, make sure to provide it with plenty of nutrients and water carefully.

**How big does Passiflora caerulea get indoors?**
Passiflora caerulea can grow quite large indoors, up to 10 feet or more, if provided with a trellis or other support. However, it can be pruned to maintain a smaller size.
