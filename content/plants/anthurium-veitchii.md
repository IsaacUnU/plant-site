## Quick Care Summary
To care for Anthurium veitchii, provide it with bright, indirect light, maintaining a distance of 3-4 feet from an east- or west-facing window. Water your plant every 7-10 days during the summer and every 14 days during the winter, making sure the soil is moist but not waterlogged. Keep the temperature between 65-80°F (18-27°C) and maintain a medium humidity level of 50-70%. With proper care, this plant is relatively easy to care for and can thrive in indoor conditions. A 1989 NASA Clean Air Study found Anthurium veitchii effective at reducing formaldehyde, xylene, and toluene in enclosed spaces, making it a great addition to any room.

## Light: The Window Direction That Determines Anthurium veitchii's Fate
The Anthurium veitchii requires bright, indirect light to thrive. The ideal light intensity is between 200-400 foot-candles, which can be achieved by placing the plant 3-4 feet away from an east- or west-facing window. Avoid placing the plant in direct sunlight, as this can cause the leaves to become scorched. If you only have a south-facing window, use a sheer curtain to filter the direct sunlight and prevent the leaves from becoming bleached.

In the winter months, when the days are shorter and the sun is lower in the sky, you may need to adjust the plant's position to ensure it receives enough light. Consider using grow lights to supplement the natural light, especially if you live in an area with limited sunlight during the winter.

## Watering: How to Water Anthurium veitchii Without Drowning It
Watering is one of the most critical aspects of caring for the Anthurium veitchii. The plant requires consistent moisture, but it's essential to avoid overwatering, which can lead to root rot. To check the soil moisture, stick your finger into the soil up to the first knuckle. If the soil feels dry, it's time to water.

Water the plant every 7-10 days during the summer and every 14 days during the winter. Use room-temperature water, and avoid getting water on the leaves to prevent fungal diseases. If you notice the plant is droopy or the leaves are yellowing, it may be a sign of overwatering. To recover from overwatering, stop watering the plant for a few weeks and allow the soil to dry out slightly.

## Soil & Repotting: The Right Soil Mix Makes Root Rot Rare
The Anthurium veitchii requires a well-draining soil mix to prevent root rot. A good soil recipe is 60% potting mix, 30% perlite, and 10% orchid bark. This mix will provide the right balance of moisture and drainage for the plant.

Repot the plant every 2-3 years to maintain soil quality and provide a larger pot if necessary. Choose a pot that is only 1-2 sizes larger than the current one, as the plant prefers to be slightly root-bound. Use a potting mix specifically designed for tropical plants, and avoid using regular potting soil, as it can retain too much water.

When repotting, gently remove the plant from its pot and inspect the roots. If the roots have become circling or tangling, use a sterile knife or pruning tool to gently trim them. This will help the plant to grow new roots and prevent it from becoming pot-bound.

## Fertilizing
Fertilize the Anthurium veitchii with a balanced 10-10-10 fertilizer during the spring and summer months. Dilute the fertilizer to half the recommended strength to avoid burning the roots. Apply the fertilizer once a month, and avoid fertilizing during the winter months when the plant is dormant.

## Humidity and Temperature
The Anthurium veitchii requires a medium humidity level of 50-70% to thrive. To maintain the ideal humidity level, you can use a pebble tray or a humidifier. Place the plant on a tray filled with water and pebbles, and as the water evaporates, it will increase the humidity around the plant.

Keep the temperature between 65-80°F (18-27°C) to prevent the plant from becoming stressed. Avoid placing the plant near heating or cooling vents, as this can cause temperature fluctuations. If you live in an area with cold winters, consider moving the plant to a warmer location during the winter months.

## Toxicity & Safety: What to Do If Your Pet Ingests the Plant
The Anthurium veitchii is non-toxic to cats and dogs, according to the ASPCA. However, it's still possible for pets to experience gastrointestinal upset if they ingest a large amount of the plant. If you suspect your pet has ingested the plant, contact the ASPCA Animal Poison Control Center at (888) 426-4435 for advice.

Keep the plant out of reach of pets to avoid any potential harm. If you have pets that like to chew on plants, consider placing the Anthurium veitchii in a hanging basket or on a high shelf.

### Leaf Drop
If the plant is dropping its leaves, it may be a sign of underwatering or overwatering. Check the soil moisture and adjust the watering schedule as needed. Make sure the plant is receiving enough light and maintain a consistent temperature.

### Root Rot
If the plant is developing root rot, it may be a sign of overwatering. Stop watering the plant for a few weeks and allow the soil to dry out slightly. Repot the plant in a well-draining soil mix and avoid getting water on the leaves.

### Pests
If you notice pests on the plant, such as spider mites or mealybugs, treat the plant with insecticidal soap. Inspect the plant regularly for pests and isolate it from other plants to prevent infestation.

## Propagation
To propagate an Anthurium veitchii, follow these steps:

1. Cut a stem section from the mother plant, making sure it has at least two nodes.
2. Remove the lower leaves from the stem section, leaving only the top two or three leaves.
3. Dip the cut end of the stem section in rooting hormone powder or liquid.
4. Plant the stem section in a well-draining soil mix, making sure the node is buried in the soil.
5. Water the soil gently but thoroughly, and provide bright, indirect light.
6. Keep the soil consistently moist but not waterlogged, and maintain a temperature of 75-85°F (24-29°C).
7. Roots should develop within 1-3 months, and the new plant can be transplanted into a larger pot.
