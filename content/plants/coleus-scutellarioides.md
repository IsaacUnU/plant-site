## Overview
Coleus scutellarioides, also known as Coleus, is a tropical plant native to Southeast Asia and Australia. It has been a popular houseplant for many years due to its vibrant, colorful foliage and relatively low maintenance requirements. Coleus scutellarioides is a member of the Lamiaceae family and is known for its striking leaves, which can range in color from deep purple to bright green. With proper care, Coleus can grow up to 3 feet tall and 2 feet wide, making it a stunning addition to any room.

In its native habitat, Coleus scutellarioides can be found growing in shady, moist areas with high humidity. However, it has adapted well to indoor conditions and can thrive in a variety of lighting conditions, from low to bright indirect light. This versatility, combined with its relatively small size and ease of care, makes Coleus scutellarioides an excellent choice for plant enthusiasts of all levels.

## Light: The Window Direction That Determines Coleus scutellarioides's Fate
Coleus scutellarioides prefers bright, indirect light, ideally between 200-400 foot-candles. Placing it near an east- or west-facing window is optimal, as it will receive gentle, indirect light for most of the day. Avoid placing it in direct sunlight, as this can cause the leaves to become scorched or bleached. In the winter months, when the days are shorter and the sun is lower in the sky, you may need to supplement with grow lights to provide the necessary light for optimal growth.

## Watering: How to Water Coleus scutellarioides Without Drowning It
To water your Coleus scutellarioides, check the soil moisture by inserting your finger into the soil up to the first knuckle. If the soil feels dry, it's time to water. Water your Coleus every 7-10 days in the summer and every 14 days in the winter, making sure not to overwater. You can also check the pot weight before and after watering to determine if the soil is dry. A moisture meter can also be used to determine the soil moisture level.

## Soil & Repotting: The Right Soil Mix Makes Root Rot Rare
To prevent root rot, use a well-draining potting mix that contains 60% potting mix, 30% perlite, and 10% orchid bark. This mix will provide the necessary drainage and aeration for the roots to thrive. When repotting, choose a pot that is only slightly larger than the previous one, as Coleus scutellarioides prefers to be slightly root-bound. Repotting should be done in the spring or summer, when the plant is actively growing.

## Fertilizing
Fertilize your Coleus scutellarioides with a balanced 10-10-10 fertilizer, diluted to half strength, once a month during the spring and summer months. This will provide the necessary nutrients for optimal growth and flowering.

## Humidity and Temperature
Coleus scutellarioides prefers a humidity level of 50-70% and a temperature range of 65-80°F (18-27°C). To achieve the right humidity level, you can use a pebble tray or humidifier. Avoid placing your Coleus near heating vents or cold windows, as this can cause the temperature to fluctuate.

## Toxicity & Safety: Is Coleus scutellarioides Safe for Your Pets?
According to the ASPCA, Coleus scutellarioides is non-toxic to cats and dogs. However, it's still important to keep it out of reach of pets, as ingestion can cause gastrointestinal upset. If you suspect your pet has ingested any part of the plant, contact the ASPCA Animal Poison Control Center at (888) 426-4435.

### Overwatering
Overwatering is a common problem with Coleus scutellarioides, as it can lead to root rot. To fix this, stop watering and allow the soil to dry out. You can also repot the plant in fresh, well-draining soil to prevent further waterlogged soil.

### Underwatering
Underwatering can cause the leaves to turn yellow and drop. To fix this, water the plant thoroughly and adjust the watering schedule to ensure it's receiving enough water.

### Pests
Pests, such as spider mites and mealybugs, can infest Coleus scutellarioides. To fix this, treat the plant with insecticidal soap or neem oil. Inspect the plant regularly for signs of pests, such as white, cottony patches or tiny, moving dots.

### Slow Growth
Slow growth can be caused by a lack of light. To fix this, move the plant to a brighter location or supplement with grow lights. Provide optimal lighting conditions to promote healthy growth.

### Droopy Leaves
Droopy leaves can be caused by low humidity. To fix this, increase the humidity with a pebble tray or humidifier. Maintain optimal humidity levels to prevent droopy leaves.

## Propagation
To propagate Coleus scutellarioides, follow these steps:
1. Cut a stem from the mother plant, just above a node.
2. Remove lower leaves, leaving only the top two or three sets of leaves.
3. Dip the cut end in rooting hormone powder or liquid.
4. Plant the stem in a pot filled with well-draining soil.
5. Water thoroughly and provide bright, indirect light.
6. Keep the soil moist but not waterlogged.
7. Roots should develop within 1-2 weeks, and the new plant can be transplanted into a larger pot.
