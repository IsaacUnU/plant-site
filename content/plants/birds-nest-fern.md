## Quick Care Summary
To care for a Bird's Nest Fern, place it in a spot with bright, indirect light, about 3-4 feet from an east- or west-facing window. Water it every 7-10 days in the summer and every 14 days in the winter, making sure the soil is moist but not waterlogged. Keep the temperature between 65-80°F (18-27°C) and maintain a medium humidity level, around 50-60%. This plant is relatively easy to care for, making it a great choice for beginners.

## Overview
The Bird's Nest Fern, also known as Asplenium australasicum, is a species of fern native to Australia and New Zealand. It's a popular houseplant due to its attractive, crinkled fronds and ability to thrive in low-light conditions. The Bird's Nest Fern is also known for its air-purifying properties, as it's able to remove pollutants like formaldehyde and xylene from the air. A 1989 NASA Clean Air Study found that this plant is effective at reducing indoor air pollution. In its natural habitat, the Bird's Nest Fern grows on tree trunks and rocky outcrops, where it receives high humidity and filtered sunlight.

The Bird's Nest Fern is a relatively small plant, growing up to 2-3 feet in height and 1-2 feet in width. Its fronds are a deep green color and have a crinkled, wavy texture. The plant is relatively slow-growing, but it's long-lived and can thrive for many years with proper care. In addition to its air-purifying properties, the Bird's Nest Fern is also a humidity-boosting plant, making it a great choice for rooms with dry air.

## Light: Choosing the Right Window Direction
The Bird's Nest Fern prefers bright, indirect light, but it can tolerate low light conditions. The ideal foot-candle range for this plant is 200-400 foot-candles. In the Northern Hemisphere, the best window orientation for this plant is east- or west-facing, as these directions provide gentle, indirect light. The plant should be placed 3-4 feet from the window to prevent scorching. In the winter, when the light is more scarce, the plant can be moved closer to the window to compensate.

## Watering: How to Water Without Drowning
To water the Bird's Nest Fern, use the finger test to check the soil moisture. Stick your finger into the soil up to the first knuckle, and if the soil feels dry, it's time to water. Water the plant every 7-10 days in the summer and every 14 days in the winter. Make sure the pot has good drainage holes to prevent waterlogging. The plant prefers filtered or rainwater at room temperature, as it's sensitive to fluoride.

## Soil & Repotting: Choosing the Right Mix
The Bird's Nest Fern prefers a well-draining potting mix to prevent root rot. A good mix can be made with 60% potting mix, 30% perlite, and 10% orchid bark. The pot material should be terracotta or ceramic, as these materials allow for good airflow and moisture transfer. The plant should be repotted every 2-3 years in the spring, when it becomes pot-bound.

## Fertilizing
The Bird's Nest Fern should be fertilized monthly during the spring and summer months with a balanced 10-10-10 fertilizer. Dilute the fertilizer to half strength to prevent burning the roots. In the fall and winter, the plant can be fertilized every 2 months, as it's dormant during this time.

## Humidity and Temperature
The Bird's Nest Fern prefers a medium humidity level, around 50-60%. To achieve this, use a pebble tray or humidifier. The plant prefers a consistent temperature between 65-80°F (18-27°C). Avoid placing the plant near heating vents or drafty windows, as this can cause temperature fluctuations.

## Toxicity & Safety: Understanding the Risks
The Bird's Nest Fern is non-toxic to cats and dogs, according to the ASPCA. However, it's still possible for pets to experience gastrointestinal upset if they ingest the plant. If you suspect your pet has ingested the plant, contact the ASPCA Animal Poison Control Center at (888) 426-4435. Keep the plant out of reach of pets to prevent accidental ingestion.

### Yellowing Leaves
Yellowing leaves can be a sign of overwatering. To fix this, reduce the watering frequency and make sure the soil is not waterlogged. Check the soil moisture by sticking your finger into the soil up to the first knuckle. If the soil feels dry, it's time to water.

### Brown Tips
Brown tips can be a sign of underwatering. To fix this, increase the watering frequency and make sure the soil is consistently moist. Water the plant when the soil feels dry, and avoid getting water on the leaves to prevent fungal diseases.

### Droopy Fronds
Droopy fronds can be a sign of low humidity. To fix this, increase the humidity around the plant. Use a pebble tray or humidifier to maintain a medium humidity level, around 50-60%.

### Pests
Pests can infest the Bird's Nest Fern, causing damage to the leaves and fronds. To fix this, treat the plant with insecticidal soap. Inspect the plant regularly for pests, and isolate the plant if you find any.

### Root Rot
Root rot can occur if the soil is waterlogged. To fix this, improve the drainage around the plant. Use a well-draining potting mix, and avoid getting water on the leaves to prevent fungal diseases.

## Propagation
To propagate the Bird's Nest Fern, follow these steps:
1. Cut a stem from the mother plant, making sure it has at least two nodes.
2. Remove lower leaves from the stem, leaving only the top two or three leaves.
3. Plant the stem in a pot filled with a well-draining potting mix.
4. Water the soil gently but thoroughly, and provide high humidity and warm temperatures.
5. Keep the soil consistently moist, but not waterlogged.
6. Roots should develop within 1-2 months, and the new plant can be transplanted into a larger pot.
