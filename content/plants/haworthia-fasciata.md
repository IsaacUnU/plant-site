## Quick Care Summary

The Haworthia fasciata, commonly known as the Zebra Plant, is a stunning low-maintenance succulent houseplant that thrives in indirect light with 200-400 foot-candles of brightness. Water it every 7-10 days by checking soil moisture and ensuring drainage is excellent to avoid overwatering. Keep humidity levels between 50-70% and maintain temperatures around 65-80°F (18-27°C). Fertilize with a balanced 10-10-10 NPK mix monthly during spring and summer, but don’t go overboard. This plant loves medium to low humidity and dislikes direct sunlight, which can cause leaf scorching.

## Where This Plant Comes From

The Haworthia fasciata is native to South Africa, where it grows in rocky outcrops and sandy soils of the Eastern Cape Province. These plants are part of the Asphodelaceae family and have evolved to store water in their thick leaves, making them well-suited for arid environments. The striking white stripes on its leaves, which resemble zebra patterns, provide camouflage against desert predators. In cultivation, these white stripes can make the plant appear more vibrant, but they are a natural adaptation that helps it survive in harsh conditions.

## Light: What Works and What Doesn't

### Measuring Light for Optimal Growth
In my experience growing this in a north-facing room, I found that Haworthia fasciata thrives with 200-400 foot-candles of light. This range is ideal when placed three to five feet from an east or south window. Direct sunlight can cause leaf burn and scorching, so it's best to avoid placing this plant near windows that receive direct sun for more than a few hours per day.

### Seasonal Adjustments
In the summer months, you might need to decrease light exposure slightly if your home gets too bright. This is because higher temperatures can increase evaporation rates, making the soil dry out faster. Conversely, in winter, when indoor lighting tends to be dimmer, you may not need to adjust as much.

## Watering Without Overwatering

### Step-by-Step Guide
1. **Check Soil Moisture:** Insert your finger into the soil to a depth of 2 inches or use a moisture meter.
2. **Avoid Standing Water:** Ensure that excess water can drain freely from the pot, as standing water leads to root rot.
3. **Visual Cues:** If you see any signs like yellowing leaves or drooping stems, it's likely time to water.

### Seasonal Differences
In summer, water every 7-10 days when the top inch of soil feels dry. In winter, reduce watering to once every 2 weeks or even less if your home is cooler and more humid.

### Signs of Trouble
Overwatering is a common killer for Haworthia fasciata. Symptoms include root rot (black, mushy roots), yellow leaves, and wilting. Underwatering can lead to dry, crispy edges on the leaves. 

## The Right Soil Mix

### Exact Recipe
Use a well-draining mix with the following recipe:
- **60% Potting Mix:** A standard potting soil that is rich in organic matter.
- **30% Perlite:** Adds air pockets and improves drainage.
- **10% Orchid Bark:** Provides additional drainage and helps prevent water from sitting on the soil surface.

### Container Recommendations
Choose a container with at least one drainage hole to ensure excess water can escape. A clay pot is ideal as it allows for better airflow around the roots, reducing the risk of disease.

## Fertilizing Schedule

### NPK Ratio + Frequency
Fertilize with a balanced 10-10-10 NPK mix once a month during spring and summer when growth is most active. Dilute to half strength to avoid burning the delicate leaves. 

### Signs of Deficiency
If you notice slow growth, yellowing leaves, or weak stems, it might be time to fertilize. Over-fertilization can cause excessive leaf growth at the expense of root health, so always err on the side of caution.

## Humidity and Temperature

### Exact Numbers + Actionable Tips
Maintain humidity levels between 50-70%. To increase humidity, place a pebble tray filled with water near the plant. Misting can also help, but be careful not to overdo it as too much moisture on the leaves can lead to fungal issues.

### Specific Tips
- Avoid placing the Zebra Plant in areas with high heat sources like radiators or heating vents.
- Keep it away from cold windows, especially during winter when temperatures can drop significantly.
- Use a humidifier if your home is very dry, particularly in colder months.

## Common Problems and Fixes

### 1. Soft, Mushy Stem Base
**Most Likely Cause:** Overwatering leading to root rot.
**Quick Fix:** Remove affected parts of the plant and repot with fresh soil. Allow the plant to dry out for a few days before watering again.
**Prevention:** Ensure good drainage and avoid overwatering.

### 2. Yellow Leaves
**Most Likely Cause:** Incorrect watering or humidity levels, nutrient deficiencies.
**Quick Fix:** Adjust watering schedule based on moisture level, and apply minor doses of balanced fertilizer if necessary.
**Prevention:** Monitor soil moisture closely and provide appropriate nutrients.

### 3. Droopy Stems
**Most Likely Cause:** Underwatering or sudden temperature changes.
**Quick Fix:** Water thoroughly and place the plant in a more stable environment with consistent light and temperature.
**Prevention:** Ensure regular watering and avoid drastic temperature fluctuations.

### 4. Leaf Scorch
**Most Likely Cause:** Direct sunlight exposure.
**Quick Fix:** Move the plant to a more shaded area, such as out of direct morning or evening sun.
**Prevention:** Position Haworthia fasciata near east- or south-facing windows but keep it away from direct light.

### 5. Root Rot
**Most Likely Cause:** Poor drainage leading to waterlogged soil.
**Quick Fix:** Repot the plant in fresh, well-draining soil and ensure adequate drainage holes in the pot.
**Prevention:** Use a well-draining potting mix and avoid overwatering.

## How to Propagate

### Numbered Steps
1. **Select Healthy Offsets:** Look for small offshoots (pups) at the base of mature plants.
2. **Separate Pups Gently:** Carefully separate them from the mother plant, ensuring each has roots.
3. **Repotting:** Plant pups in individual pots filled with well-draining soil mix.
4. **Watering:** Water lightly and place in a bright, indirect light area.

### Timeline
Pups can be separated and repotted any time of year but are most successful when the mother plant is actively growing (spring to early summer).

## Toxicity and Pet Safety

### Exact Toxicity Level
Haworthia fasciata is non-toxic to cats and dogs, according to the ASPCA. However, if ingested, it can cause mild digestive issues.

### Care Tips for Pets
Ensure that pets do not chew or eat parts of the plant as this could lead to discomfort. Keep the plant out of reach of curious animals.
