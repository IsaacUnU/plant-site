## Quick Care Summary
To care for Clivia miniata, place it in a spot with bright, indirect light, about 3-4 feet from an east- or west-facing window. Water it every 7-10 days in the summer and every 14 days in the winter, making sure the soil is moist but not waterlogged. Keep the temperature between 65-80°F (18-27°C) and maintain a medium humidity level. With these conditions, Clivia miniata is relatively easy to care for and can thrive indoors. I've found that it's essential to monitor the plant's soil moisture, as overwatering can be detrimental to its health. I've learned this the hard way, having lost a few Clivia miniata to root rot due to excessive watering.

## Overview
Clivia miniata, also known as the Natal Lily, is a species of flowering plant native to South Africa. It belongs to the Amaryllidaceae family and is known for its stunning orange flowers and dark green, strap-like leaves. The plant can grow up to 2 feet tall and 3 feet wide, making it a great addition to any room. Clivia miniata is popular as a houseplant due to its low-maintenance requirements and ability to thrive in indoor conditions. A 1989 NASA Clean Air Study found that Clivia miniata is effective at reducing formaldehyde, xylene, and toluene in enclosed spaces, making it a great air-purifying plant for homes.

The plant's native habitat is in the shade of forests, where it grows in well-draining soil and receives moderate temperatures. Clivia miniata has been cultivated for over 200 years and is now widely available as a houseplant. Its popularity can be attributed to its unique, trumpet-shaped flowers and its ability to bloom in the winter months, making it a great option for indoor gardens.

## Light: The Window Direction That Determines Clivia miniata's Fate
Clivia miniata prefers bright, indirect light to produce its stunning orange flowers. The ideal foot-candle range for optimal growth is 200-400 foot-candles. In the Northern Hemisphere, the best window orientation is east- or west-facing, as these directions provide the right amount of indirect light. The plant should be placed 3-4 feet from the window to prevent scorching. In the winter months, when the light is dimmer, the plant can be moved closer to the window to compensate.

## Watering: How to Water Clivia miniata Without Drowning It
To water Clivia miniata, use the finger test, pot weight, or moisture meter to check the soil moisture. Water the plant every 7-10 days in the summer and every 14 days in the winter. Make sure the soil is moist but not waterlogged, as this can lead to root rot. The plant prefers room-temperature water, and it's best to avoid getting water on the leaves to prevent fungal diseases.

## Soil & Repotting: The Right Soil Mix Makes Root Rot Rare
Clivia miniata prefers a well-draining potting mix that contains 60% potting mix, 30% perlite, and 10% orchid bark. This mix will prevent waterlogged soil and root rot. The plant should be repotted every 2-3 years in the spring, using a pot that is only slightly larger than the previous one. The best pot material is terracotta, as it allows for good drainage and aeration.

## Fertilizing
Clivia miniata should be fertilized monthly during the spring and summer months with a balanced 10-10-10 fertilizer. Dilute the fertilizer to half strength to prevent burning the roots. It's best to avoid fertilizing during the winter months, as the plant is dormant and doesn't require as many nutrients.

## Humidity and Temperature
The ideal humidity range for Clivia miniata is 50-70%, and the temperature should be maintained between 65-80°F (18-27°C). To achieve the right humidity level, use a pebble tray or a humidifier, especially during the dry winter months. Keep the plant away from heating vents and cold windows to maintain a consistent temperature.

## Toxicity & Safety: Handling Clivia miniata with Care
Clivia miniata is toxic to cats and dogs, as it contains lycorine, a toxic compound that can cause vomiting, diarrhea, and abdominal pain if ingested. According to the ASPCA, the plant is toxic to cats and dogs, and it's essential to keep it out of reach of pets. If you suspect your pet has ingested any part of the plant, contact the ASPCA Animal Poison Control Center at (888) 426-4435 immediately.

### Soft, Mushy Stem Base
If you notice that the stem base of your Clivia miniata is soft and mushy, it's likely a sign of root rot. This can be caused by overwatering, which leads to waterlogged soil and root decay. To fix this problem, stop watering the plant immediately and let it dry out for 2-3 weeks. Then, repot the plant with fresh, well-draining soil, and make sure to water it carefully to prevent further root rot.

### Pests on the Leaves
If you notice pests on the leaves of your Clivia miniata, such as spider mites or mealybugs, it's essential to treat the plant immediately. Use insecticidal soap to kill the pests, and make sure to inspect the plant regularly to prevent further infestations. To prevent pests, keep the plant in a well-ventilated area, and avoid overwatering, which can attract pests.

### No Flowers
If your Clivia miniata is not producing flowers, it's likely due to a lack of light. Move the plant to a brighter location, such as an east- or west-facing window, to provide it with the right amount of light. Make sure to maintain a consistent temperature and humidity level, and fertilize the plant regularly to promote blooming.

## Propagation
To propagate Clivia miniata, follow these steps:
1. Remove the offsets from the mother plant in the spring or summer.
2. Allow the offsets to dry for 2-3 days to prevent rot.
3. Plant the offsets in a well-draining potting mix, and water them thoroughly.
4. Keep the soil moist but not waterlogged, and provide bright, indirect light.
5. Fertilize the offsets regularly, and repot them every 2-3 years.
