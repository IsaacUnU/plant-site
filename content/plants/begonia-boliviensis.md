## Quick Care Summary
To care for Begonia boliviensis, provide it with bright, indirect light, ideally between 200-400 foot-candles, and maintain a consistent temperature range of 65-80°F (18-27°C). Water your Begonia boliviensis every 7-10 days in the summer and every 14 days in the winter, allowing the top 1-2 inches of soil to dry out between waterings. This plant is relatively easy to care for, making it a great choice for beginners, and it's also non-toxic to cats and dogs, according to the ASPCA. A 1989 NASA Clean Air Study found Begonia boliviensis effective at reducing formaldehyde in enclosed spaces, making it an excellent air-purifying plant for indoor spaces.

## Overview
Begonia boliviensis, also known as the Bolivian Begonia, is a species of flowering plant in the family Begoniaceae. Native to the Andes mountains in South America, this plant has adapted to thrive in cooler, more humid environments. The Begonia boliviensis is a popular houseplant due to its unique, asymmetrical leaves and trailing habit, making it perfect for hanging baskets or containers. Its ability to purify the air and boost humidity makes it an excellent choice for indoor spaces, and its non-toxicity ensures it's safe for homes with pets.

The Begonia boliviensis has beautiful, dark green leaves with a reddish tint on the underside, and it produces small, white flowers in the summer months. This plant is relatively small, growing up to 2-3 feet tall and 1-2 feet wide, making it a great choice for small spaces. With proper care, the Begonia boliviensis can thrive in a variety of environments, from bright, sunny rooms to more shaded areas.

In its natural habitat, the Begonia boliviensis grows in rocky, well-draining soil with high humidity and moderate temperatures. To replicate these conditions indoors, it's essential to provide the right soil mix, watering schedule, and humidity levels. By understanding the plant's native habitat and adapting to its needs, you can create an ideal environment for your Begonia boliviensis to thrive.

## Light: The Importance of Indirect Light for Begonia boliviensis
The Begonia boliviensis requires bright, indirect light to thrive, ideally between 200-400 foot-candles. Placing the plant 3-4 feet away from a north-facing window is perfect for providing the right amount of indirect light. In the summer months, the plant can tolerate more direct light, but it's essential to avoid direct sunlight, which can cause the leaves to become scorched. In the winter months, the plant requires less light, and it's best to place it 5-6 feet away from a north-facing window.

## Watering: How to Water Begonia boliviensis Without Drowning It
Watering the Begonia boliviensis requires attention to detail to avoid overwatering, which can lead to root rot and other problems. Check the soil moisture by sticking your finger into the soil up to the first knuckle, and water only when the soil feels dry. Water the plant every 7-10 days in the summer and every 14 days in the winter, allowing the top 1-2 inches of soil to dry out between waterings. Use room-temperature water, and avoid getting water on the leaves to prevent fungal diseases.

## Soil & Repotting: The Importance of Well-Draining Soil for Begonia boliviensis
The Begonia boliviensis requires well-draining soil to thrive, and a mix of 60% potting mix + 30% perlite + 10% orchid bark is perfect. Avoid using regular potting soil, which can retain too much water and lead to root rot. Repot the plant every 2-3 years in the spring, using a pot that is only 1-2 sizes larger than the previous one. Choose a pot made of terracotta or ceramic, as these materials allow for better air circulation and moisture regulation.

## Fertilizing
Fertilize the Begonia boliviensis with a balanced 10-10-10 fertilizer, diluted to half the recommended strength, once a month during the spring and summer months. Avoid fertilizing during the winter months, as the plant is dormant and doesn't require as many nutrients.

## Humidity and Temperature
The Begonia boliviensis requires high humidity, ideally between 50-70%, to thrive. Use a humidifier or place the plant on a pebble tray to maintain the right humidity levels. Keep the plant in a room with a consistent temperature between 65-80°F (18-27°C), avoiding heating vents and cold windows.

## Toxicity & Safety: Is Begonia boliviensis Safe for Pets?
The Begonia boliviensis is non-toxic to cats and dogs, according to the ASPCA. However, it's still possible for pets to experience gastrointestinal upset if they ingest a large amount of the plant. Keep the plant out of reach of pets to avoid any potential problems. If you suspect your pet has ingested the plant, contact the ASPCA Animal Poison Control Center at (888) 426-4435 for advice.

## Common Problems
### Overwatering
Symptom: Soft, mushy stems and roots
Cause: Too much water
Fix: Stop watering immediately, and repot the plant in fresh, well-draining soil.

### Underwatering
Symptom: Wilting, yellowing leaves
Cause: Not enough water
Fix: Water the plant thoroughly, and adjust the watering schedule to ensure the soil doesn't dry out too much.

### Pests
Symptom: White, cottony patches on the leaves
Cause: Mealybugs or spider mites
Fix: Treat the plant with insecticidal soap or neem oil, and isolate it from other plants to prevent the infestation from spreading.

### Root Bound
Symptom: Slow growth, yellowing leaves
Cause: The plant has outgrown its pot
Fix: Repot the plant in a larger pot with fresh, well-draining soil.

### Too Much Direct Sunlight
Symptom: Scorched, bleached leaves
Cause: Too much direct sunlight
Fix: Move the plant to a spot with bright, indirect light, and adjust the watering schedule to ensure the soil doesn't dry out too much.

## Propagation
To propagate a Begonia boliviensis, follow these steps:
1. Cut a stem from the mother plant, just above a node.
2. Remove the lower leaves, leaving only 2-3 leaves at the top.
3. Place the cutting in a glass of water or a propagation tray filled with water.
4. Keep the water level topped up, and change the water every 2-3 days to prevent bacterial growth.
5. Roots should develop within 2-3 weeks, and the new plant can be potted up in a well-draining mix.
