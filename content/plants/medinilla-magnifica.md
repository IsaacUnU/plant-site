## Quick Care Summary
To care for Medinilla magnifica, place it in a spot with bright, indirect light, about 3-4 feet away from an east- or west-facing window. Water it every 7-10 days in the summer and every 14 days in the winter, making sure the soil is moist but not waterlogged. Keep the temperature between 65-80°F (18-27°C) and maintain a medium humidity level of 50-70%. This plant is relatively easy to care for and can thrive in indoor conditions with minimal maintenance.

## Light: The Importance of Bright, Indirect Light
Medinilla magnifica requires bright, indirect light to thrive. The ideal light intensity is between 200-400 foot-candles, which can be achieved by placing the plant near an east- or west-facing window, about 3-4 feet away. Avoid placing the plant in direct sunlight, as it can cause the leaves to become scorched. In the winter months, when the light is less intense, you can place the plant closer to the window to ensure it receives enough light.

## Watering: How to Water Medinilla magnifica Without Drowning It
Watering Medinilla magnifica can be a bit tricky, as it requires moist soil but can be prone to root rot if the soil is too waterlogged. To check the soil moisture, stick your finger into the soil up to the first knuckle. If the soil feels dry, it's time to water. Water the plant every 7-10 days in the summer and every 14 days in the winter. Use room-temperature water, and avoid getting water on the leaves to prevent fungal diseases.

## Soil & Repotting: The Importance of Well-Draining Soil
Medinilla magnifica requires well-draining soil to prevent root rot. A good potting mix can be made by combining 60% potting mix, 30% perlite, and 10% orchid bark. Use a terracotta pot, as it allows for better air circulation and can help prevent root rot. Repot the plant every 2-3 years, using a pot that is only slightly larger than the previous one. Signs that the plant needs repotting include roots growing out of the drainage holes or the plant becoming top-heavy.

## Fertilizing
Feed Medinilla magnifica with a balanced 10-10-10 fertilizer during the spring and summer months. Dilute the fertilizer to half strength to avoid burning the roots. Apply the fertilizer once a month, and avoid fertilizing during the winter months when the plant is dormant.

## Humidity and Temperature
Medinilla magnifica requires a medium humidity level of 50-70% to thrive. To maintain the ideal humidity level, use a pebble tray or a humidifier. Keep the temperature between 65-80°F (18-27°C), and avoid placing the plant near heating vents or cold windows.

## Toxicity & Safety: Non-Toxic to Cats and Dogs
According to the ASPCA, Medinilla magnifica is non-toxic to cats and dogs. However, it's still important to keep the plant out of reach of pets to avoid any potential harm. If you suspect your pet has ingested any part of the plant, contact the ASPCA Animal Poison Control Center at (888) 426-4435 for advice.

### Yellowing Leaves
Yellowing leaves can be caused by overwatering, which can lead to root rot. To fix this issue, stop watering the plant for 2-3 weeks and allow the soil to dry out completely. Then, resume watering the plant regularly, making sure not to overwater.

### Droopy Stems
Droopy stems can be caused by underwatering, which can cause the plant to become stressed. To fix this issue, water the plant immediately and make sure the soil is moist but not waterlogged.

### Brown Edges on Leaves
Brown edges on leaves can be caused by too much direct sunlight, which can cause the leaves to become scorched. To fix this issue, move the plant to a spot with bright, indirect light, and use a sheer curtain to filter the sunlight.

### Root Rot
Root rot can be caused by waterlogged soil, which can lead to fungal diseases. To fix this issue, repot the plant in well-draining soil and avoid overwatering.

### Pests
Pests, such as spider mites or mealybugs, can infest the plant and cause damage. To fix this issue, treat the plant with insecticidal soap and inspect the plant regularly for pests.

## Propagation
To propagate Medinilla magnifica, follow these steps:
1. Cut a stem section from the mother plant, making sure it has at least two nodes.
2. Remove the lower leaves, leaving only the top two or three leaves.
3. Dip the cut end in rooting hormone powder or liquid.
4. Plant the stem section in a pot filled with well-draining soil, making sure the node is buried in the soil.
5. Water the soil gently and keep it moist but not waterlogged.
6. Place the pot in a warm, humid location with bright, indirect light.
7. Roots should develop within 2-3 months, and the new plant can be transplanted into a larger pot.
